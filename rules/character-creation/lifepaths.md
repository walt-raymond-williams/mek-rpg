# Lifepaths And Life Modules

## Purpose

Use this summary to build a character history through Life Modules: affiliation, childhood, education, training, and real-life experience before play begins.

## When to Use

- A player wants character creation to produce both mechanics and background.
- The GM needs to check whether a PC's affiliation, era, education, military training, or career path is coherent.
- A character must choose stage-based modules, skill fields, prerequisites, or flexible XP assignments.
- The table wants campaign hooks from a character's upbringing, school, service, career, or faction ties.

## Do Not Use For

- Copying module entries, affiliation lists, school lists, skill-field catalogs, or sample builds into committed files.
- Points-only builds except as inspiration for background. Use `rules/character-creation/overview.md` and `rules/character-creation/attributes.md`.
- Exact XP costs or module awards. Inspect the cited private source pages.
- Post-creation advancement. Use `rules/character-creation/xp-advancement.md`.

## Basic Procedure

1. Confirm the campaign's allowed era, affiliations, and character types before selecting modules.
2. Choose Stage 0 affiliation. This establishes cultural origin, language assumptions, initial traits or skills, later restrictions, and possible sub-affiliation detail.
3. Apply the universal starting requirements for Life Module characters from the source pages, including baseline attribute, language, and perception purchases.
4. Choose one Stage 1 early-childhood module and one Stage 2 late-childhood module if the character continues through later stages.
5. Choose optional Stage 3 higher-education modules when the character's concept requires schooling, military or police training, technical education, professional credentials, or skill fields.
6. Choose optional Stage 4 real-life modules for careers, tours of duty, work experience, wandering, or other pre-play adult history.
7. For each selected module, subtract its XP cost from the starting XP pool and assign its fixed and flexible XP to the listed attributes, traits, and skills.
8. Track prerequisites throughout creation. They can often be satisfied by later choices, but the finished character must meet them unless the GM approves an exception.
9. Determine final levels only after all module XP, flexible XP, optimization, and final purchases are applied.
10. Convert the selected modules into playable background: home, school, service, mentors, enemies, obligations, faction loyalties, and reasons to join the campaign.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Ask for the character's intended role first, then use modules to explain how they reached it.
- Stage 3 and Stage 4 choices can make older, more specialized characters. Check whether the character's age and experience still fit the campaign.
- Skill Fields are training bundles, not a license to ignore prerequisites. They are most useful when a PC needs a recognizable military, technical, professional, or Clan role.
- If a player wants a concept that module prerequisites block, decide openly whether the campaign benefits from a GM exception.
- Do not let module catalogs slow a session. During live setup, record likely choices and mark exact XP/table lookup as a character-creation work item.

## Common Edge Cases

- Changing affiliations can represent defection, adoption, capture, migration, or changed adult allegiance. Use the source procedure and restrictions rather than improvising XP math.
- A repeated or conflicting requirement should be resolved with the stricter prerequisite unless the source or GM says otherwise.
- Some subskills are chosen from open categories such as a language, affiliation, environment, or `Any` field. Record the exact subskill on the sheet.
- Repeating real-life modules may not grant all benefits again. Use source pages for exact repeat handling.
- Clan, caste, phenotype, and affiliation restrictions are dense and table-heavy. Use page references instead of summarizing every restriction.
