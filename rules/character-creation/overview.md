# Character Creation Overview

## Purpose

Use this summary to run or review the foundation of A Time of War character creation without relying on memory. Character creation builds a PC or unique NPC from a campaign concept, an XP budget, attributes, traits, skills, background choices, and starting equipment.

## When to Use

- A player wants to create a new PC for a campaign.
- The GM needs to decide whether a proposed concept fits the campaign era, affiliation limits, character-type limits, and party role.
- A character sheet needs a completeness review before play.
- The table needs to choose between the Life Modules method and the points-only method.

## Do Not Use For

- Running a full character build from copied module, trait, skill, or equipment tables. Use the cited private source pages for exact catalog entries and XP tables.
- Tactical BattleTech pilot ratings or unit conversion. Use `rules/vehicles-and-mechs/` and `gm/switch-to-classic-battletech.md` once those summaries are source-reviewed.
- Ordinary action resolution after play begins. Use `indexes/task-router.md` and the relevant core, combat, equipment, or campaign summary.
- Long-term advancement after the character has entered play. Use `rules/character-creation/xp-advancement.md` and `rules/campaign/advancement.md`.

## Basic Procedure

1. Establish the campaign frame with the GM: campaign type, era, allowed affiliations, character-type limits, expected combat scale, and any table restrictions.
2. Choose a character concept that fits the campaign and the other PCs. Define the role in plain language before spending XP.
3. Choose the creation method:
   - Life Modules for an organic history that generates background, affiliations, education, career stages, attributes, traits, and skills.
   - Points-only creation for faster NPCs, custom concepts, or cases where the module list does not fit.
4. Start from the campaign's XP allotment. The standard baseline is 5,000 XP for a normal starting human, with age adjustments and Clan-warrior baseline differences handled from the source pages when they matter.
5. Build the mechanical core: attributes, traits, skills, prerequisites, and any remaining XP allocation.
6. Add final touches: physical description, homeworld, affiliation, background, motivations, contacts or obligations implied by traits, and personal equipment.
7. Complete the character record with attributes, linked modifiers, trait points and page references, skills with levels and linked data, combat data, inventory, and any vehicle or assigned-unit notes.
8. Before play, mark unresolved sheet items as `Needs source review`, `Needs GM ruling`, or `Needs user decision`.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Start from the campaign premise. A good character for a mercenary DropShip crew may be a poor fit for a Clan warrior trial, a police procedural, or a Solaris intrigue campaign.
- Life Modules are useful because they create hooks. Convert module choices into NPCs, debts, training institutions, affiliations, enemies, family ties, and mission expectations.
- Points-only characters still need a backstory. Use Life Module descriptions as inspiration even when the mechanics are purchased directly.
- Keep exact module rewards, skill fields, trait lists, and sample character details in the private source. The committed summary should tell the GM what to look up and how to use it.
- Treat character creation as table setup, not a private math exercise. The GM should know why each PC is in the group and what kinds of scenes they are built to support.

## Common Edge Cases

- Prerequisites for modules, skill fields, traits, and attributes do not always need to be satisfied before a choice is selected, but the completed character must satisfy them unless the GM intentionally waives them.
- Conflicting prerequisites can invalidate a later choice. When two requirements compete, use the stricter requirement or ask the GM to approve an exception.
- Negative traits can add XP during creation, but the source caps this and requires the chosen drawbacks to fit the character. Do not use negative traits as pure accounting.
- Older characters may trigger advancement or aging rules after character creation rather than during the starting allotment step.
- Starting equipment is controlled by Wealth, Equipped, affiliation, legality, availability, and optional issued-gear decisions. Use `rules/equipment/overview.md` for equipment procedure and source pages for exact items.
