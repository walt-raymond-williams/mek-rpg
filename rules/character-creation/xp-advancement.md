# XP Advancement

## Purpose

Use this summary to route experience awards, post-creation improvement, training, downtime advancement, aging effects, and money/rank consequences after a character enters play.

## When to Use

- The GM is awarding XP after a session, mission, campaign step, roleplaying moment, or major achievement.
- A character wants to spend XP to improve attributes, Edge, traits, or skills.
- The campaign has downtime and characters may practice, train, work, or improve.
- A character's age, rank, salary, wealth, property, or responsibilities matter after creation.

## Do Not Use For

- Initial character-build math unless the build references age effects or leftover XP. Use `rules/character-creation/overview.md`.
- Exact XP award tables, aging table, XP cost table, salary table, bonus table, or monthly expense table. Inspect the cited private source pages.
- Campaign contracts, reputation, contacts, downtime consequences, or injury recovery beyond advancement routing. Use future campaign summaries.
- Tactical BattleTech advancement, unit logistics, or MekHQ accounting.

## Basic Procedure

1. After play, run a short feedback pass. Identify group performance, mission progress, campaign progress, individual character development, roleplaying, achievements, and table entertainment.
2. Award XP conservatively. Ordinary sessions should not produce excessive XP; major awards should correspond to meaningful risk, progress, or standout play.
3. Record earned XP in the active campaign save, usually in `campaigns/<campaign-id>/pcs.md` and session notes.
4. To spend XP, identify whether the improvement is an attribute, Edge, trait, skill, or campaign resource.
5. For attributes and Edge, spend the required XP and require a plausible practice, training, or campaign explanation.
6. For traits, require GM approval and a clear fictional reason. Some traits are creation-only or can only change through specific events.
7. For skills, check whether the skill is Basic or Advanced, whether a successful use can justify improvement, and whether a trainer is required.
8. For downtime, calculate possible downtime XP or training progress from the source pages, then record what the character did during that time.
9. For wealth, salary, rank, property, and bonuses, route to campaign systems and the cited source tables rather than inventing numbers.

## Practical GM Guidance

- XP is a pacing tool. Reward behavior and campaign movement you want to see repeated.
- Give feedback close to the session so players understand what mattered, but avoid exposing every hidden campaign signal through XP accounting.
- Advancement should be visible in the fiction. Training, mentors, scars, responsibilities, debts, and role changes make mechanical growth feel earned.
- Do not let one player's personal subplot consume the entire table. Handle extended individual advancement scenes outside the main session when needed.
- Rank and income advancement can change the campaign scale. Decide early whether promotions, command authority, and salaries are central to the campaign.

## Common Edge Cases

- Aging can alter attributes and traits. Check the age table and phenotype or Clan-specific notes before applying any effect.
- Advanced skills require training support even if a character has enough XP recorded.
- Downtime XP is slow and may depend on Intelligence, learner traits, career skill use, and available time.
- Negative traits gained during play do not automatically pay the same as creation-time negative traits. Award XP only when the GM wants the drawback to matter.
- Salary, bonuses, expenses, and rank can add campaign bookkeeping. Keep it light unless the campaign is about unit management or logistics.
