# Traits

## Purpose

Use this summary to choose, record, interpret, and advance traits that give characters special advantages, disadvantages, resources, obligations, identities, or vehicle-related features.

## When to Use

- A character receives traits from Life Modules or buys traits during points-only creation.
- A player wants to take negative traits to gain additional creation XP.
- The GM needs to adjudicate identity-based, multiple, opposing, flexible, vehicle, or variable-level traits.
- A campaign event might add, remove, worsen, or justify improving a trait.

## Do Not Use For

- Copying the master trait list, trait tables, random unit assignment tables, or full trait descriptions into committed files.
- Exact trait point ranges, equipment ratings, income tables, rank tables, random vehicle/unit results, or trait-specific modifiers. Inspect the cited private source pages.
- Ordinary skill or attribute checks unless a specific trait modifies the situation. Use core summaries first.
- Vehicle/BattleMech tactical resolution. Use `gm/switch-to-classic-battletech.md` and future vehicle summaries.

## Basic Procedure

1. Identify how the trait enters the build: Life Module award, direct purchase, negative-trait XP, post-creation advancement, injury, campaign reward, or GM consequence.
2. Record the trait's name, trait point value, whether it is positive, negative, flexible, neutral, multiple, identity-based, opposed, restricted, character-only, or vehicle-only.
3. For multiple or identity-based traits, add a plain-language descriptor that says which alias, contact, enemy, obligation, body part, vehicle, property, organization, or secret it belongs to.
4. Check opposing traits. If the source says two traits oppose each other, cancel or offset them according to the specific trait rule.
5. For variable-level traits, use the private source table to choose the level and effect. Do not infer higher or lower effects from the trait name alone.
6. During character creation, apply negative-trait XP only within the source limit and only when the drawback fits the concept and campaign.
7. During play, require fiction and GM approval before adding, improving, buying off, or worsening a trait.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Traits are campaign handles. Connections, enemies, rank, wealth, property, reputations, obligations, secrets, and dependents should create future scenes.
- Negative traits should create meaningful constraints, not invisible rebates. Define when they can appear before play starts.
- Do not hide important identity-based trait bookkeeping. If a PC has aliases, separate which resources and liabilities belong to each identity.
- Vehicle traits can imply assigned or owned assets, but exact unit identity, rating, and tactical use need source and tactical-system review.
- Some traits are mainly character-creation gates. If a trait can only be acquired during creation, do not add it later without an explicit campaign exception.

## Common Edge Cases

- A trait may be fully paid for in XP before its effect activates. Partial progress should be recorded but not treated as active.
- Multiple versions of the same trait can be distinct if their descriptors differ. Do not merge them unless the source and GM permit it.
- Flexible traits can be positive or negative. Record the sign clearly so XP math and fictional implications stay visible.
- Traits tied to equipment, wealth, rank, or property can interact with starting gear and campaign resources. Use equipment and campaign summaries for downstream procedures.
- Injuries, surgery, implants, prosthetics, aging, and social consequences can alter traits during play and may require campaign-consequence summaries.
