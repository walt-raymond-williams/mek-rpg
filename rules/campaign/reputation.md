# Reputation

## Purpose

Use this summary when public standing, faction opinion, known achievements, scandal, rank, title, property, or past behavior changes how people treat a character or unit.

## When to Use

- A mission outcome changes what an employer, faction, settlement, unit, or public audience thinks of the PCs.
- A PC's Reputation, Rank, Title, Property, Wealth, Enemy, Connection, or similar trait affects a scene.
- The GM needs to decide whether prior actions alter access, prices, trust, hostility, legal attention, command support, or job offers.
- Secret deeds might become rumors, evidence, blackmail, or delayed public consequences.

## Do Not Use For

- One-off NPC attitude with no durable audience. Record it in `npcs.md` or resolve a social check.
- Calling on a specific helper or network. Use `rules/campaign/contacts.md`.
- Exact Reputation trait point values, property administration modifiers, rank/title equivalencies, or title/rank trait suggestions. Inspect the cited private source pages.
- Universal reputation. Standing should be tied to an audience.

## Basic Procedure

1. Identify the audience: employer, faction, local population, military unit, noble court, criminal network, media public, Clan society, or a specific NPC circle.
2. Identify what the audience knows. Separate public facts, rumors, secrets, classified actions, and false reports.
3. Decide whether the effect is immediate attitude, a trait-backed modifier, a future opportunity, a restriction, an enemy reaction, or a campaign hook.
4. If a source trait applies, check the private source for exact scope and modifier before using a number.
5. Record the change in `factions.md` and `relationships.md`; add named NPC carriers in `npcs.md` when useful.
6. If reputation affects mission offers, contract terms, prices, salary/bonus, rank, or legal access, update `missions.md`, `assets.md`, and `hooks.md`.
7. Keep contradictory reputations if different audiences would reasonably disagree.

## Practical GM Guidance

Make reputation specific and recent. "The Galatea yard crews trust them after the rescue" is more useful than "good reputation +1." Tie the effect to who heard what, what they can do about it, and what they expect next.

Use reputation as a pressure valve. A good name can open doors and create demands; a bad name can close one employer while attracting another.

## Common Edge Cases

- Secret success may produce no public reputation until witnesses, records, or rumors surface.
- Public success can still hurt standing with a rival faction.
- A title or rank may impress one culture and create suspicion in another.
- Property scandals, military failures, or political disasters can damage standing even when the character was not personally present.
- Reputation can support contacts and contracts, but should not replace the need to negotiate or pay costs.
