# Advancement And Rewards

## Purpose

Use this summary at the end of a session, mission, downtime interval, or campaign milestone when the GM needs to award XP, handle character improvement, apply aging effects, resolve training progress, or decide whether money, property, salary, bonuses, rank, or power should change persistent campaign state.

## When to Use

- The table is closing a session and needs XP feedback.
- A mission, campaign objective, roleplaying moment, or standout achievement deserves a reward.
- A player wants to spend earned XP on attributes, Edge, traits, or skills.
- A character is training, practicing during downtime, using a Career Skill during downtime, or looking for a mentor.
- Aging, salary, expenses, bonuses, rank, property, or command responsibility matters to the next scene.

## Do Not Use For

- Initial character creation or points-only build cleanup. Use `rules/character-creation/overview.md` and `rules/character-creation/xp-advancement.md`.
- Exact XP award values, aging effects, XP cost values, salary values, bonus percentages, housing/food/utility costs, or table examples. Inspect the cited private source pages.
- Exact title equivalencies, military rank lists, Clan status lists, or social-rank tables. Use `rules/campaign/power-rank-and-title.md` and inspect the cited source pages.
- Mercenary-company hard-ledger accounting, MekHQ payroll, force maintenance, BattleMech repair costs, or tactical campaign logistics.

## Basic Procedure

1. Hold a short feedback pass after play. Ask what mattered, name group progress, call out individual development, and avoid turning the conversation into hidden score optimization.
2. Separate group rewards from individual rewards. Group rewards usually reflect mission and campaign progress; individual rewards reflect character development, roleplaying, rare achievements, and table-positive entertainment.
3. Use the private award table only when exact XP matters. Keep normal-session XP modest and reserve exceptional awards for genuinely rare moments.
4. Record earned XP, unspent XP, and the reason for the award in the active campaign save, usually `pcs.md` and `session-log.md`.
5. When XP is spent, identify the target improvement: attribute, Edge, trait, Basic Skill, Advanced Skill, or a campaign-facing resource.
6. Require a fictional basis for improvement. Practice, field use, a mentor, a facility, medical recovery, faction support, or downtime can justify the change.
7. For skills, determine whether the character qualifies through successful use or requires training. Advanced Skills require training; Basic Skills can improve through use or training.
8. For training, confirm the trainer is better than the student in the target skill, set the time commitment, adjust for learner traits and facility quality, then make the Training check after the full interval.
9. During downtime, decide whether the character is working, training, studying, practicing, recovering, or doing nothing consequential. Career Skill use and focused effort can matter; recreation or inaction should produce little advancement.
10. For aging, salary, expenses, bonuses, property, rank, and power, inspect the cited table pages when exact values matter, then record the campaign consequence and obligation rather than only the number.

## Practical GM Guidance

XP is feedback, pacing, and permission to grow. Tell players the broad reasons they advanced, but do not reveal every scoring knob if that would teach them to chase a table instead of playing the campaign.

Use non-XP rewards when they fit better: a contact, a repair window, a safehouse, a promotion, a command, a salary change, a debt, an enemy, or access to property can move the campaign more than a point award.

Rank, property, and wealth should create obligations. A promotion can bring subordinates, rival officers, superior orders, audits, public scrutiny, and enemies. Decide deliberately whether the campaign wants that larger scale.

## Common Edge Cases

- A normal session should not flood characters with XP. If the campaign should start with powerful PCs, use a larger starting XP budget rather than inflated routine awards.
- Character development can deserve individual XP, but a single PC subplot can also pull the session away from mission or campaign progress. Reward the growth while protecting table focus.
- A high roll is not automatically an achievement. Reward the rare moment where the roll, narration, risk, and consequence combine into something memorable.
- Aging can change attributes or traits at birthday thresholds. Apply the table exactly from the private source when the birthday matters.
- Edge can be improved like an attribute, and spent Edge can be replenished with XP; check the cited pages for the exact cost.
- Negative traits inflicted by play do not automatically grant creation-style XP. Award XP for a new drawback only when the GM wants that drawback to matter.
- Interrupted training can resume later, but the check waits until the required full interval has elapsed.
- Salary and bonus handling can become bookkeeping-heavy. Use it when employment terms, unit politics, debt, or command responsibility are foreground play.
