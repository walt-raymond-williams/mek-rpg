# Transport And Large Assets

## Purpose

Use this summary to frame RPG scenes about acquiring, inspecting, controlling, fueling, crewing, repairing, or recording transport and large campaign assets such as personal vehicles, support vehicles, spacecraft access, DropShip stakes, assigned unit transport, or valuable property.

This is a campaign procedure, not a DropShip economy. It uses A Time of War support for acquisition pressure, vehicle access, skills, repair framing, wealth/property hooks, and campaign consequences while preserving known gaps around exact prices, title law, and force-level logistics.

## When to Use

- A character buys, requisitions, borrows, inherits, salvages, leases, or negotiates access to a significant vehicle or transport asset.
- A DropShip, spacecraft berth, shuttle, vehicle convoy, or other large asset changes the mission plan.
- The table needs an inspection, repair, lockout, legal review, crew-availability, permit, fuel, or readiness scene.
- The GM needs to record ownership, custody, debts, defects, obligations, or unresolved source lookups in the active campaign save.

## Do Not Use For

- Exact DropShip prices, purchase tables, operating costs, docking fees, fuel values, cargo income, loan schedules, or crew payroll. Inspect private source material or use table canon, MekHQ, or another campaign-economy tool.
- Definitive title-transfer law, liens, registration, insurance, port permits, customs, cooperative shares, or legal ownership procedure. Treat these as setting/table rulings unless a later source is added.
- Full tactical aerospace, DropShip, BattleMech, or vehicle combat. Use Classic BattleTech, MegaMek, MekHQ, or the tactical handoff.
- Direct MekHQ ledger, roster, repair, market, or calendar edits. For MekHQ-linked campaigns, record pending intent in MEK-RPG and apply hard changes in MekHQ.

## Basic Procedure

1. Define the asset and scene scale: personal vehicle, support vehicle, combat vehicle, aerospace craft, spacecraft access, DropShip stake, cargo berth, assigned transport, property, or abstract patron support.
2. Identify the current control claim: owned, assigned, borrowed, leased, captured, salvaged, collateralized, disputed, faction-controlled, or promised but not delivered.
3. Set the acquisition route: normal purchase, special order, requisition, patron favor, contract term, salvage award, black-market deal, inheritance, loan, title transfer, or hostile recovery.
4. Check pressure points before rolling: local availability, legality, faction scrutiny, time, debt, condition, missing documents, known defects, remote locks, crew access, fuel/readiness, and whether the asset creates tactical-tool work.
5. Resolve only meaningful uncertainty with the normal skill-check route. Common rolls include negotiation, bureaucracy, streetwise sourcing, technical inspection, security bypass, repair triage, legal/social pressure, or crew persuasion.
6. Record the outcome as campaign state, not hidden narration: asset name, type, location, controller, owner claim, legal status, condition, fuel/readiness, crew needs, debts/liens/obligations, faction interest, and unresolved lookup gaps.
7. If the asset becomes a tactical unit, stop before improvising full BattleTech mechanics. Prepare a handoff packet with crew roles, known condition, mission objective, and consequences to bring back into campaign files afterward.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Large assets are story pressure. A transport asset can solve travel, create debt, attract rivals, expose paperwork gaps, or force the crew to choose between speed, secrecy, legality, and safety.
- Treat price and ownership as campaign facts only when a source, campaign save, or table ruling has established them. Otherwise, save the open question explicitly.
- Inspection scenes should produce actionable defects: unsafe now, safe but costly, legal but watched, operational with a hidden lockout, repairable with parts, or useful only as leverage.
- Crew is part of readiness. Record who can fly, maintain, command, inspect, negotiate for, or legally represent the asset.
- Do not punish the players with invisible accounting. Put costs, debts, deadlines, and administrative risks where the players can make choices about them.

## Common Edge Cases

- A clean bill of sale does not prove practical control if the asset has a remote lock, missing crew, bad maintenance, hostile port authority, or faction claim.
- Salvage is not automatically ownership. Record who recognizes the claim, who contests it, and what tool or ruling will settle the next step.
- A patron-provided DropShip or transport solves access but creates obligations. Track who can revoke it and under what conditions.
- Fuel and maintenance can be abstract readiness clocks unless exact values matter. If exact values matter, route to the private source, table canon, or MekHQ.
- A character trait, rank, wealth, property, or faction tie can justify access, but the exact trait catalog remains a source lookup.
- For MekHQ-linked campaigns, MEK-RPG can remember promises, scene consequences, and pending actions; MekHQ owns force accounting, repairs, markets, unit rosters, dates, and hard ledger changes.
