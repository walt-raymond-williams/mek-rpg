# Prosthetics And Implants

## Purpose

Use this summary when lost limbs, sensory replacements, organ replacements, cloned replacements, bionic prosthetics, elective myomer implants, or Enhanced Imaging implants affect a character sheet or campaign consequence.

## When to Use

- A PC or NPC loses a limb, eye, ear, organ, or other body part and needs a replacement.
- A player asks how a prosthetic offsets a Lost Limb, Poor Vision, Poor Hearing, or Handicap trait.
- Surgery, recovery time, physical therapy, repair, damage to a replacement, or medical access matters.
- Elective implants or Enhanced Imaging are requested, discovered, removed, damaged, or used in tactical play.

## Do Not Use For

- Real-world medical advice about prosthetics, implants, surgery, addiction, neurological effects, or recovery.
- Reproducing prosthetic, bionic, cloned replacement, elective implant, or Enhanced Imaging tables.
- Granting enhanced body modifications without source review, surgery requirements, recovery, drawbacks, and table consent.
- Tactical Enhanced Imaging effects without source or tool lookup.

## Basic Procedure

1. Identify whether the replacement is restorative or elective.
2. For restorative replacements, identify the lost body part and the trait it currently creates or modifies.
3. Look up the replacement type in the source when exact offset, BAR, cost, availability, surgery, or repair detail matters.
4. Record whether the replacement is removable, grafted/cloned, external, internal, sensory, or organ-related.
5. Resolve installation through Surgery and facility requirements from the source. Record physical therapy or recovery time before reducing sheet penalties.
6. If a replacement is damaged, use the source's BAR/damage handling and note whether the character suffers fatigue, ordinary damage, disability, or replacement destruction.
7. For elective myomer or Enhanced Imaging implants, verify surgery, recovery, addiction/dependency, future medical penalties, tactical benefits, and long-term deterioration before approving.

## Live Play Procedure

1. Start from the injury or elective request. Name the affected body part, current trait consequence, and whether the player is trying to restore function, conceal damage, or gain an enhancement.
2. Confirm consent and campaign tone for surgery, disability, addiction, and body modification before making the option central to play.
3. Choose the broad replacement path: simple removable prosthetic, advanced/bionic replacement, cloned/grafted replacement, elective myomer implant, or Enhanced Imaging implant.
4. Use the cited source pages for exact trait offset, BAR, surgery requirements, downtime, cost, availability, and side effects. Do not improvise table values.
5. During recovery, track facility access, medical supervision, physical therapy, debt or patron obligation, and whether the character is mission-ready at reduced capability.
6. If the replacement is damaged, confiscated, removed, or medically complicated, treat it as both an equipment problem and an injury consequence. Route repair or surgery through medical gear and injury recovery.
7. For Enhanced Imaging or other tactical-facing implants, prepare the benefit and drawback in a tactical handoff packet instead of inventing combat modifiers during play.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Treat prosthetics as injury aftermath, not a casual shopping upgrade. They can restore function, create debt, require therapy, and change how a character is perceived.
- A replacement may reduce a trait's effect without deleting the underlying story or equipment dependency.
- Cloned replacements are a special case and may remove the underlying lost-part trait, but cost, access, time, and culture can make them unavailable.
- Elective implants are high-risk and high-consequence. They should be rare, campaign-visible, and source-checked.
- Enhanced Imaging is mostly tactical-facing. If it matters in a battle, prepare it in the handoff packet rather than improvising benefits.

## Common Edge Cases

- A removable replacement can be lost, disabled, confiscated, damaged, or unavailable in a prison or medical scene.
- Internal replacements require surgery for removal or repair.
- Cosmetic treatment may hide a prosthetic but does not make access or maintenance disappear.
- Future medical treatment on implanted areas can be harder; record the complication.
- Elective implants can create addiction, recovery delay, doubled healing time, or permanent consequences that should be explicit before installation.
