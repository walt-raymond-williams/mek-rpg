# Drugs And Poisons

## Purpose

Use this summary when medicines, performance enhancers, narcotics, poisons, toxins, antidotes, addiction, or chemical exposure affect RPG play.

## When to Use

- A character is dosed, poisoned, sedated, intoxicated, drugged, exposed to toxins, treated with antidote, or prescribed medicine.
- A medic uses medication, boosters, antibiotics, anti-toxin, or field treatment.
- A player asks about performance-enhancing drugs, narcotics, addiction risk, withdrawal, treatment, or legality.
- A creature venom, disease treatment, hostile atmosphere, chemical weapon, or mission hazard routes to drug/poison mechanics.

## Do Not Use For

- Real-world medical, toxicology, drug-use, addiction, dosing, or treatment advice.
- Copying sample drug/poison tables, drug creation tables, poison values, doses, or chemical weapon details.
- Encouraging real-world drug use or poison creation.
- Replacing consent and safety discussions for addiction, poisoning, coercion, or medical trauma themes.

## Basic Procedure

1. Identify the category: medicine, performance enhancer, narcotic, poison, venom, toxin, antidote, or created compound.
2. Identify the route of exposure in game terms: injected, ingested, inhaled, contact, or other source-supported vector.
3. If exact effects matter, inspect the cited source for strength, duration, damage type, protection, addiction risk, legality, price, and equipment ratings.
4. For medicines, use MedTech and the relevant recovery/disease procedure. Failed medical use can under-dose, over-dose, interact badly, or worsen the problem if the source supports it.
5. For poisons, resolve whether the toxin enters the body, whether protective gear applies, and whether a BOD check resists it.
6. For poison treatment, identify whether a specific antidote exists or broad treatment can only stabilize the patient.
7. For narcotics or performance enhancers, track active effects, crash/side effects, addiction checks, Compulsion trait consequences, and recovery requirements only from source-supported procedure.
8. Record medical state, remaining doses, legality, addiction risk, treatment plan, and unresolved source checks in campaign files.

## Live Play Procedure

1. Establish fictional stakes first: accidental exposure, medical treatment, creature venom, deliberate poisoning, contraband, performance enhancer, withdrawal, or chemical-war hazard.
2. Name the vector and protection before rolling. Sealed gear, filter masks, full-body clothing, vehicles, and ordinary armor do not protect against the same threats.
3. If the substance has a known source entry, use its cited private table values for strength, duration, damage, AP, addiction, treatment, and legality. If it is fictional or newly created, pause for source lookup rather than inventing chemistry.
4. For poison or venom, resolve whether it enters the body, make the appropriate resistance check from the source procedure, and turn ongoing harm into a visible clock.
5. For treatment, distinguish a true antidote from broad stabilization. Use MedTech, facility access, suitable kits, and follow-up care as the main play levers.
6. For narcotics and performance enhancers, make the short-term benefit, side effect, legal risk, and addiction check trigger explicit before use.
7. For addiction or withdrawal arcs, record the substance, Compulsion or recovery consequence, treatment requirement, and table-consent boundaries in campaign notes.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Keep this procedural and fictional. The summary is for game outcomes, not real chemistry or medical instructions.
- Drugs and poisons are sensitive content. State the stakes, keep agency clear, and avoid coercive or graphic treatment unless the table wants that tone.
- Performance enhancers should have meaningful drawbacks and legality risks, not just bonuses.
- Toxins and venoms are often time clocks. Tell the players whether they need immediate stabilization, a proper antidote, hospital care, or evacuation.
- Chemical warfare, nerve agents, and similar threats should be rare, heavily consequential, and routed as mission hazards or war-crime stakes rather than casual gear.

## Common Edge Cases

- Protective gear can block some vectors but not others. A filter mask, sealed suit, full-body clothing, battle armor, and vehicle environmental sealing are different protections.
- A medicine can act like a poison after an error, allergy, interaction, fumble, or deliberate misuse.
- Addiction checks need not happen after every use; the GM can call for them when dramatically appropriate and source-supported.
- An antidote may stabilize, halt spread, or cure depending on treatment success and source details.
- Drug creation is a table-heavy subsystem. Do not run it from summary memory; use the private source and table consent.
