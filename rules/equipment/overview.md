# Equipment Overview

## Purpose

Use this summary to decide whether a character can obtain, carry, maintain, and use personal-scale gear during RPG scenes. It covers availability, legality, cost pressure, equipment condition, core equipment data terms, and repair basics.

## When to Use

- A player wants to buy, requisition, scavenge, trade, steal, or black-market personal equipment.
- The GM needs a quick ruling on whether gear is locally available, legal, suspicious, damaged, powered, loaded, or maintainable.
- Exact gear stats are needed and the group needs to know which private source pages to inspect.
- A character is repairing personal equipment or personal armor outside full vehicle or BattleMech maintenance rules.

## Do Not Use For

- Tactical BattleMech, vehicle, aerospace, or full battlefield equipment resolution. Use the tactical handoff and the relevant BattleTech system.
- Exact equipment table values, item-by-item catalogs, or stat blocks. Inspect the cited private source pages.
- Character Wealth Trait, Equipped Trait, or lifestyle rules beyond their role in deciding access to gear.
- Long-term campaign inventory accounting unless the campaign wants that level of logistics.

## Basic Procedure

1. Identify the gear category: weapon, armor, electronics, medical gear, survival/tool gear, or special-purpose gear.
2. Decide whether the item is a normal local purchase, a special order, a faction or military issue item, a gray-market find, black-market contraband, salvage, or stolen property.
3. Check the local context: technology level, era, faction, settlement size, legality, and whether the character has Wealth, Equipped, credentials, contacts, or a plausible source.
4. If the item has exact game stats, look up the table on the cited private source pages instead of recreating values in the summary.
5. Record the table-facing details that affect play: skill used, cost, mass, ammo or power needs, range, armor/barrier values, shots, reload limits, crew requirements, notes, and affiliation restrictions.
6. For used, improvised, damaged, or poorly maintained gear, decide whether to apply a situational modifier, increase fumble risk, limit reliability, or require a repair check before use.
7. For repairs, require appropriate tools and a relevant Technician or specialty skill check when failure matters. Personal armor repair also needs suitable patches or replacement materials.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Treat equipment as a permission and consequence layer, not a shopping minigame by default. Ask what the player is trying to accomplish, then choose the narrowest gear lookup that answers it.
- Use legality and visibility as scene texture. A military rifle, sealed environment suit, or surveillance kit may be mechanically available but socially risky.
- Keep exact item stats off the chat transcript unless the player is reading their own legal source. The summary should say where to look and how to use the result.
- Let contacts, faction credentials, salvage rights, and local reputation matter. A mercenary on a contract, a criminal in a port district, and a tourist in a secure arcology should not see the same marketplace.
- If a gear question would slow the scene, make a temporary ruling, mark it for source review, and continue.

## Common Edge Cases

- Black-market acquisition may require Streetwise, Negotiation, contacts, or similar scene work before money can solve the problem.
- Used or damaged equipment can be cheaper but unreliable. Use failure consequences, fumble handling, or repair requirements rather than inventing hidden table values.
- Equipment ratings and affiliation notes can mean an item is regionally rare, restricted, or culturally specific even when the item type is familiar.
- Lacking proper tools can impose a major repair penalty or make a repair impossible.
- BattleMech, vehicle, and aerospace repair should leave this summary and use the tactical or maintenance-focused source material.
