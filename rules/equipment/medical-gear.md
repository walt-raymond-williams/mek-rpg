# Medical Gear

## Purpose

Use this summary to connect medical equipment, field treatment supplies, stimulants, preservation gear, and life-support devices to personal combat recovery and emergency care.

## When to Use

- A character treats wounds, bleeding, shock, fatigue, unconsciousness, poison, surgery needs, or evacuation risk using gear.
- The GM needs to know whether field-care, surgical, monitoring, stimulant, preservation, or life-support equipment can plausibly help.
- A medic wants to stabilize someone in the field, prepare transport, reduce penalties, or attempt more advanced care.
- The exact device effect needs a private source page reference.

## Do Not Use For

- Replacing healing, stabilization, surgery, or recovery procedures. Use `rules/personal-combat/recovery.md`.
- Exact doses, bonuses, penalties, duration, cost, mass, power use, or table notes. Inspect the cited private source pages.
- Long-term campaign injury tracking beyond immediate medical support.
- Treating medical gear as a guaranteed cure when the recovery rules call for rolls, time, facilities, or consequences.

## Basic Procedure

1. Identify the medical problem: bleeding, wound stabilization, fatigue, unconsciousness, poison or toxin exposure, surgery, transport, monitoring, life support, or long-term recovery.
2. Read the relevant recovery summary first, then decide which gear can assist that procedure.
3. Select the broad gear category: field-care kit, surgical support, monitoring device, wound-care consumable, stimulant, preservation equipment, or life-support equipment.
4. Look up exact table effects only when they matter to the roll or consequence.
5. Choose the relevant skill: MedTech for field care, Surgery for invasive treatment, or another medical specialty if the scene calls for it.
6. Apply the equipment effect, consumable use, power requirement, time, and facility limits from the source entry.
7. Record remaining supplies, addiction or side-effect concerns, transport condition, and whether follow-up care is still needed.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Medical gear should buy time, reduce risk, or enable care. It usually should not erase the original injury from the story.
- Field treatment and clinical treatment are different. A good field kit can stabilize someone, but surgery and recovery may still need safer conditions.
- Stimulants are useful under pressure but should carry later costs if the source notes addiction, crash, fatigue, or medical risk.
- Preservation and life-support gear are excellent evacuation tools. Make the evacuation route, time pressure, and power/supply limits clear.
- If a table value is needed, pause for source lookup rather than inventing a dose or modifier.

## Common Edge Cases

- A kit may be necessary for a medical roll but not sufficient by itself. Skill, time, conditions, and patient state still matter.
- Some devices monitor or preserve rather than heal. Do not turn diagnostic information into automatic recovery.
- Repeated stimulant use can create campaign consequences.
- Battlefield or vehicle-scale medical facilities may belong in future campaign or tactical support summaries.
- Unknown or exotic toxins, radiation, or disease may require source review or a custom ruling.
