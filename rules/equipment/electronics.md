# Electronics

## Purpose

Use this summary for communications, computers, diagnostics, audio/video equipment, surveillance devices, optics, remote sensors, and related personal-scale electronic systems.

## When to Use

- A scene involves radios, headsets, relays, jamming, intercepted signals, microcomms, or shared tactical communication.
- Characters use computers, diagnostic tools, control interfaces, mapping displays, or verification equipment.
- A character deploys bugs, microphones, bug scanners, cameras, optics, rangefinders, night-vision, infrared, motion sensors, seismic sensors, or other remote sensors.
- The GM needs to decide what skill reads, jams, finds, spoofs, or interprets an electronic device.

## Do Not Use For

- Exact ranges, costs, power use, mass, table bonuses, or item notes. Inspect the cited private source pages.
- Vehicle, aerospace, BattleMech, battlefield ECM, or Total Warfare sensor procedures.
- Cybernetics, advanced implants, or setting-specific computer systems outside the mapped source pages.
- Treating an electronic device as automatic proof or automatic detection when opposition or uncertainty matters.

## Basic Procedure

1. Identify the electronic function: communication, recording, display, computing, diagnostic aid, surveillance, counter-surveillance, optic enhancement, remote sensing, or monitoring.
2. Check whether the device has enough power, range, network access, relay support, compatible frequency, line of sight, or environmental suitability.
3. Look up exact table values only when they matter to the ruling.
4. Choose the relevant skill: Communications, Sensor Operations, Perception, Security Systems, Technician, Science, or another appropriate specialty.
5. For opposed detection, define what is being hidden or detected: sound, heat, movement, radar return, seismic vibration, visual image, data signal, or transmitter.
6. Apply interference, terrain, metal, ECM, distance, stealth gear, concealment, and operator attention as situational modifiers.
7. Resolve the scene through core skill checks or opposed actions. Use tactical rules if the device is interacting with battlefield-scale sensors or ECM.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Comms make squads more effective only when the whole group can actually communicate on the same working net.
- Relays, local infrastructure, jamming, and terrain are good levers. They can preserve useful comms while still making a mission feel dangerous.
- Sensors are not magic vision. They answer specific questions and can be fooled, blocked, overwhelmed, or misread.
- Diagnostic gear should support a technical task, not replace the Technician or Science roll when failure matters.
- Surveillance gear creates an evidence trail. Decide who can discover, trace, jam, spoof, or later audit it.

## Common Edge Cases

- A single team member with working comms does not automatically give the whole group a communication benefit.
- Cities and high-tech facilities may have relay infrastructure; wilderness, underground areas, heavy structures, or hostile ECM may not.
- Remote sensors may detect without giving precise identification. Heat, movement, radar, and seismic cues each answer different questions.
- Sensor setup and sensor interpretation can be different tasks with different skills and timing.
- Tactical ECM and vehicle-scale sensor rules are outside this summary. Hand off when unit-scale combat or battlefield detection matters.
