# Armor And Protection

## Purpose

Use this summary to adjudicate personal armor, shields, protective clothing, hostile-environment gear, stealth gear, and the boundary between personal protection and tactical armor systems.

## When to Use

- A character buys, wears, repairs, conceals, or relies on personal armor.
- An attack hits protected body locations or interacts with armor, barriers, shields, or hostile-environment suits.
- The GM needs to decide whether protective gear is legal, socially acceptable, bulky, compromised, or suitable for the hazard.
- A character uses camouflage, thermal masking, ECM-style protection, or other stealth gear against personal-scale detection.

## Do Not Use For

- Exact armor BAR values, cost, mass, patch cost, coverage, or item notes. Inspect the cited private source pages.
- Battle armor, exoskeleton, BattleMech, vehicle, or aerospace tactical stat resolution.
- Replacing personal combat damage, wound, barrier, or tactical handoff rules.
- Copying standard kit tables or faction loadout lists into committed files.

## Basic Procedure

1. Identify the protective item: personal armor, helmet, shield, gloves, boots, load-bearing gear, hostile-environment suit, stealth gear, neurohelmet, battle armor, or exoskeleton.
2. Look up exact table values only when needed: BAR by damage type, coverage, mass, cost, repair patch, affiliation, and notes.
3. Resolve attacks through personal combat damage. Apply armor and barrier handling from the damage summary and source table result.
4. If optional hit locations are not being used, follow the source's simplified armor handling rather than treating every armor piece as equally relevant.
5. For hostile-environment gear, define the hazard first: vacuum, hostile atmosphere, temperature, toxins, underwater exposure, bright light, darkness, or sensory conditions.
6. For stealth gear, identify which detection channel is being opposed and route the roll through Stealth, Sensor Operations, Perception, or the tactical system as appropriate.
7. For battle armor or exoskeletons, decide whether the scene is still personal-scale. If vehicles, battlefield weapons, armor facings, or tactical movement matter, hand off.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Armor is not invisible. Heavy, military, or sealed protection should affect social scenes, checkpoints, stealth, fatigue, and escalation.
- Use environment suits as a binary fiction-first tool: they protect while intact and appropriate, but damage or poor fit can turn the hazard back on.
- Shields behave more like barriers than worn armor. Route them through the relevant barrier/cover logic.
- Stealth equipment should not replace a stealth scene. It changes detection chances and channels, but patrol behavior, lighting, sensors, and cover still matter.
- Standard faction kits are useful as flavor and preparation shortcuts. Use page references for exact kit contents.

## Common Edge Cases

- Some protective clothing has modest protective value but should not be treated like purpose-built combat armor.
- Suit compromise matters for sealed or hazardous environments. If the suit loses its protective integrity, resolve the environment as an active threat.
- Stealth modifiers against personal sensors and optics differ from tactical vehicle sensor rules. Use the correct scale.
- Neurohelmets are critical for some vehicle and BattleMech roles, but their detailed use belongs with vehicle or tactical procedures.
- Battle armor and exoskeletons sit on the boundary. Personal combat can handle infantry-scale interactions; tactical combat should handle unit-scale or vehicle-scale exchanges.
