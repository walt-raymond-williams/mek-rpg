# Weapons

## Purpose

Use this summary to choose and adjudicate personal-scale weapons, ammunition, accessories, grenades, mines, demolitions, and support weapons without reproducing equipment tables.

## When to Use

- A character attacks with a melee weapon, thrown weapon, small arm, support weapon, grenade, mine, explosive charge, or special ammunition.
- The GM needs to decide which weapon skill, range, ammunition, reload, power, crew, or accessory detail matters.
- A player wants to equip a character for a mission and needs the right private source page for exact stats.

## Do Not Use For

- Exact weapon AP, base damage, range, cost, mass, shots, reload, or table notes. Inspect the cited private source pages.
- BattleMech-scale, vehicle-scale, aerospace, or full tactical combat weapon resolution.
- Building exhaustive weapon catalogs in committed project files.
- Real-world weapon simulation beyond the source rules.

## Basic Procedure

1. Identify the weapon category: archaic melee, modern melee, thrown, bow or archaic ranged, small arm, support weapon, explosive, ordnance, ammunition type, or accessory.
2. Pick the matching attack procedure from personal combat: ranged attacks, melee attacks, thrown attacks, indirect fire, demolitions, or a tactical handoff if the scene has left RPG-scale combat.
3. Look up the weapon's private table entry when exact AP, damage, range, shots, reload, cost, mass, skill, affiliation, or notes matter.
4. Apply ammunition, ordnance, and accessory notes after the base weapon is identified. Specialty ammunition can replace or modify attack codes, but do not infer changes beyond the source entry.
5. Track practical limits: reloads, power packs, ammunition supply, concealment, legality, noise, crew, setup time, back-blast, minimum range, and collateral damage.
6. Resolve the attack through the appropriate combat summary, then resolve damage through the damage and armor summaries.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Ask for the weapon's table row only when a stat will change the outcome. For simple scene framing, broad category and legality may be enough.
- Support weapons should feel heavy, visible, and logistically awkward. Crew, setup, transport, ammunition, and legal attention are usually as important as damage.
- Explosives are scene-shaping tools. Clarify placement, timing, cover, blast exposure, and noncombat consequences before rolling.
- Specialty ammunition and accessories should answer a mission problem, not become a free stack of bonuses. Use the source notes and stop there.
- If tactical units, vehicles, BattleMechs, armor facings, heat, or map-scale range brackets matter, switch to the tactical system.

## Common Edge Cases

- Crew-served weapons can often be assembled or operated by fewer people, but missing crew slows or complicates use. Do not let extra helpers exceed the source limits.
- Grenades and mortars interact with indirect fire, scatter, range, and ordnance details. Use personal combat for character-scale throws and the source pages for exact payload effects.
- Mines and demolition charges need setup choices and appropriate skill checks. Fumbles can be dangerous.
- Recoilless weapons have back-blast hazards. Do not ignore who or what is behind the shooter.
- "Less-lethal" weapons can still create serious consequences through stun, fatigue, poison, gas, or environmental risk.
- Guided, gas, inferno, smoke, stun, flare, and similar ordnance types need source lookup for exact effects.
