# Planetary Conditions

## Purpose

Use this summary when terrain, weather, gravity, atmosphere, vacuum, or other environmental conditions affect RPG-scale movement, attacks, survival, tactical handoff, or mission planning.

## When to Use

- A mission takes place in difficult terrain, bad weather, darkness, smoke, unusual gravity, tainted atmosphere, vacuum, water, magma, unstable ground, or extreme temperatures.
- A character's movement, visibility, fatigue, falling risk, swimming, climbing, exposure, protective gear, or ranged attack is affected by the environment.
- The GM needs to decide whether a terrain or weather condition stays in personal combat or must be handed to Total Warfare, Tactical Operations, Strategic Operations, MegaMek, or MekHQ.
- Environmental conditions create a campaign clock: shelter, oxygen, suit breach, heat/cold exposure, flood, avalanche, quake, or storm.

## Do Not Use For

- Reproducing movement-cost tables, terrain modifier tables, atmospheric categories, or exact tactical terrain rules.
- Full vehicle, BattleMech, aerospace, or tactical battlefield resolution. Use the tactical handoff and external tactical rules.
- Real-world survival, medical, or environmental safety advice.
- Inventing exact planetary science where a campaign world profile or tactical source should decide.

## Basic Procedure

1. Identify the active environmental layer:
   - terrain type
   - terrain condition
   - dynamic hazard
   - weather or visibility
   - temperature
   - gravity
   - atmosphere or vacuum
2. Decide the scene scale. Personal movement and survival can usually stay in RPG mode; vehicle movement, terrain MP, line of sight, and tactical unit effects should hand off.
3. State the mechanical pressure before rolling: slower movement, attack penalty, RFL check, Swimming check, BOD check, exposure damage, suit breach, fall, entrapment, or forced movement.
4. Apply only source-supported modifiers. If exact terrain or weather values matter, inspect the cited pages or use the tactical source/tool.
5. Treat protective gear as conditional. It helps only if it is appropriate, intact, powered or supplied, and not breached.
6. Track continuing hazards at the End Phase or scene clock: fire, heat/cold, vacuum, taint, smoke, water current, disease exposure, or structural collapse.
7. Record persistent consequences in the active campaign save: injury, gear damage, lost supplies, delayed arrival, damaged vehicle, exposed position, or follow-up medical care.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Stack only the environmental pressures that matter to the decision. A blizzard may imply cold, wind, snow, visibility, fatigue, and navigation risk, but not every subrule needs a live roll.
- For characters, use the environment to create choices: slow down, spend gear, seek shelter, split up, risk a shortcut, call for extraction, or trigger tactical handoff.
- For vehicles and BattleMechs, terrain and weather can become exact tactical concerns quickly. Do not approximate terrain MP, skid risk, aerospace movement, or building damage from memory.
- Suit breaches and hostile atmospheres are high-stakes. Make the available protection, supply clock, repair option, and retreat route clear.
- Gravity and atmosphere can affect encumbrance, fatigue, movement, RFL checks, and long-term traits. Mark long-term consequences as source-review items before altering a character sheet.

## Common Edge Cases

- Terrain types and terrain conditions can combine. Muddy woods, deep snow on ice, smoke in darkness, and high wind during rain should be treated as layered hazards.
- Buildings can be cover, barriers, tactical structures, and collapse hazards. Use `rules/combat/armor-and-barriers.md` for personal barrier logic and tactical tools for structural combat.
- Water can be cover, movement terrain, drowning pressure, current, diving, or vehicle terrain depending on depth and equipment.
- Vacuum and deadly taints require sealed protection. Ordinary clothing or partial masks are not enough unless the source says they are.
- Extreme heat/cold and magma are not just movement terrain; they can create continuous damage or fatigue/exposure clocks.
