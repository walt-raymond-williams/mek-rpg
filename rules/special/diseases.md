# Diseases

## Purpose

Use this summary when illness, failed inoculation, alien disease, medical maintenance, recovery, or a disease-driven plot affects campaign play.

## When to Use

- Characters travel to a new world and need inoculation, medical screening, or disease-prevention logistics.
- A PC or NPC becomes ill and the illness affects Attributes, traits, mission timing, quarantine, treatment access, or recovery.
- The GM wants a disease as a plot clock, mystery, or treatment quest.
- Illness interacts with medical gear, MedTech, hospital access, Clan phenotype, downtime, or long-term character consequences.

## Do Not Use For

- Real-world medical advice, diagnosis, treatment, epidemiology, or medication guidance.
- Copying random disease tables, symptom lists, severity/frequency tables, or examples.
- Surprise-killing PCs with unavoidable disease. The source explicitly frames disease as something to use carefully.
- Replacing personal combat damage or poison rules.

## Basic Procedure

1. Decide whether disease belongs in the campaign. For ordinary travel, it can be abstracted as routine inoculation or medical maintenance.
2. If prevention matters, identify who provides inoculation or screening: military command, employer, ship medical staff, spaceport clinic, local physician, or improvised medic.
3. If an illness appears, define its function: inconvenience, mission pressure, mystery, quarantine, race for a cure, or lasting consequence.
4. Choose or source-review symptoms, frequency, severity, and affected Attributes/Traits. Do not copy table entries into campaign notes; paraphrase the effect.
5. Use BOD-based recovery and MedTech-supported treatment only from the cited source. Record medicine, antibiotics, cures, facility quality, and failed-treatment consequences as source lookup facts.
6. If an Attribute reaches zero or below, use the core zero-attribute effect routing and mark any permanent effect as source-reviewed before altering the sheet.
7. Track recovery and mission consequences in `pcs.md`, `npcs.md`, `current-state.md`, `missions.md`, and `hooks.md`.

## Live Play Procedure

1. Choose the disease's table purpose before choosing mechanics: travel logistics, quarantine pressure, mystery investigation, a race for treatment, local ecology, or long-term consequence.
2. Decide prevention first. If the characters had access to military, employer, ship, spaceport, or clinic inoculation, resolve that before springing symptoms.
3. If symptoms matter, choose or roll symptom, frequency, and severity privately. Commit only a paraphrased effect and source reference to campaign notes.
4. Convert the illness into clocks the table can act on: next symptom interval, recovery-check cadence, treatment search, quarantine deadline, mission delay, or worsening Attribute/trait consequence.
5. Resolve treatment with MedTech, medical kit/facility access, and the source recovery procedure. Do not invent medication bonuses or cure availability.
6. After successful recovery, track natural recovery of disease-caused losses separately from zero-attribute or permanent consequences, which may require XP, therapy, or special treatment.
7. If the disease affects public safety, record the social fallout: quarantine order, port denial, employer pressure, local stigma, or faction investigation.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Disease is usually optional. It works best when it creates choices: find a doctor, buy time, quarantine, pursue a cure, abandon a route, or risk a mission while sick.
- Make disease clocks visible enough for players to act. Hidden deterioration can work for mystery, but not as an unavoidable punishment.
- Inoculation is a logistics scene, not a full medical subsystem. Use cost, time, access, and failure consequences only when they matter.
- Use sensitive narration. Focus on symptoms, risk, care, and choices rather than graphic detail.
- If a disease should be fatal without treatment, make a cure, treatment path, or meaningful rescue route available unless the table explicitly wants bleak medical risk.

## Common Edge Cases

- Successful inoculation is not perfect immunity. It lowers risk but does not eliminate unknown strains or unusual exposure.
- Fumbled medical prevention can create allergic or local-ecosphere complications; exact effects require source review.
- Clan phenotype can affect illness recovery checks; verify exact application in the source.
- Asymptomatic or delayed illness can be a plot hook, but record uncertainty and avoid retroactive gotchas that remove player agency.
- A disease can affect traits such as vision, hearing, paralysis, or compulsions; update the sheet only after source lookup and table consent where appropriate.
- Random disease tables are inspiration and procedure, not a mandate to surprise the party. Choose results when a specific story or medical clock is better than randomness.
- Attribute and trait losses can cascade into derived stats, skill links, movement, damage capacity, and social consequences. Recalculate only the fields that matter now, then record the unresolved cleanup.
- Fatal disease should have a visible path to care, cure, containment, or informed risk unless the table explicitly wants bleak medical stakes.
