# Damage

## Purpose

Damage rules translate a successful attack, fall, hazard, or other harm into standard damage, fatigue, armor interaction, stun, and follow-up wound checks.

## When to Use

- A ranged or melee attack hits.
- A character falls, burns, bleeds, drowns, suffocates, is poisoned, or suffers another personal-scale hazard.
- The GM needs to apply armor or barrier reduction before marking damage.
- The scene needs to know whether the character is stunned, fatigued, unconscious, bleeding, or dead.

## Do Not Use For

- Vehicle, BattleMech, aerospace, or full tactical armor-location damage.
- Equipment catalog lookup for exact weapon, armor, or medical gear values.
- Optional hit locations or armor degradation unless those optional/details rules are active and checked from the cited source.
- Copying damage notation tables, standard damage tables, or armor tables into committed notes.

## Basic Procedure

1. Identify the damage source and its damage notation or source-defined damage formula.
2. Determine whether the harm is standard damage, fatigue damage, or continuous damage.
3. For attacks, use the attack's margin and attack type to adjust damage according to the source procedure.
4. Apply armor or barrier effects when the damage type and protection apply.
5. Mark standard damage against the character's standard damage capacity and fatigue against fatigue capacity.
6. When standard damage is inflicted by an attack, add the source-defined fatigue side effect unless the attack already deals fatigue.
7. Apply stun when the triggering damage type calls for it.
8. Make consciousness or bleeding checks when required by the wound rules.
9. If continuous damage applies, track it for the End Phase.

## Practical GM Guidance

At the table, resolve damage in one clear pass: source, armor, marked damage, immediate condition, then ongoing effects. Avoid diving into every adjacent optional rule unless the result depends on it.

For table-driven details, stop and inspect the cited page. This summary is meant to keep the flow straight, not replace the source's damage and armor tables.

## Common Edge Cases

- Standard damage represents physical injury and can kill. Fatigue represents exhaustion, subduing harm, and other nonlethal impairment.
- If fatigue exceeds its capacity, the character falls unconscious and excess fatigue spills into standard damage.
- Ranged and melee attacks that cause damage also normally cause stun.
- Area-effect attacks use proximity to the impact point; burst fire can add damage based on margin and shots fired.
- Continuous damage usually resolves in later End Phases, not only when the attack first hits.
- Armor protects only when its barrier rating exceeds the attack's armor penetration in the relevant damage type; detailed armor and barrier handling is table-heavy and source-cited.
- Falling damage uses a source formula based on distance and surface; short uncontrolled falls may still stun.
