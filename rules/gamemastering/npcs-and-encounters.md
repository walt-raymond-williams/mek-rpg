# NPCs And Encounters

## Purpose

Use this routing aid when creating or adjusting NPCs, deciding how much detail an NPC needs, balancing NPC competence against the PCs, or using random encounters as campaign pressure.

## When to Use

- The GM needs a quick NPC for a scene.
- A named NPC must roll, fight, command resources, or challenge a PC.
- A group of enemies, allies, contacts, subordinates, or authorities must be scaled to the party.
- A random encounter or complication should be adapted to the current mission.

## Do Not Use For

- Reproducing NPC templates, stat blocks, equipment kits, random encounter tables, or ratio tables.
- Creating final PC-grade characters. Use character creation summaries.
- Treating random encounter results as mandatory events instead of prompts.
- Full tactical encounter force design. Use tactical handoff tools.

## Basic Procedure

1. Decide how important the NPC is:
   - color-only NPC: name, role, attitude, one useful detail
   - functional NPC: enough attributes, skills, traits, and gear for likely checks
   - major NPC: campaign motive, resources, relationships, and consequences
2. Set the NPC relative to a relevant PC or party role. Decide whether the NPC should be below, near, above, or far above PC competence.
3. Assign only the stats needed for the scene. If an exact template matters, inspect the cited source pages.
4. Choose the NPC's attitude toward the PCs: friendly, hostile, neutral, pressured, conflicted, or transactional.
5. Give equipment based on scene function and resources, not only personal competence.
6. For groups, check whether the encounter should challenge, delay, reveal, assist, or redirect the PCs.
7. Record recurring or consequential NPCs in the active campaign save.

## Practical GM Guidance

- Roll for NPCs when the result can surprise the table and improve play. Do not roll just to watch scripted outcomes happen.
- NPC power should serve the scene. A powerful patron, a weak but well-connected clerk, and an ordinary guard can all matter in different ways.
- Random encounters work best when tied to current obligations, local authorities, rivals, resources, or mission clocks.
- If a random encounter would derail the campaign, translate it into a clue, pressure, cost, or future hook instead of forcing the full event.
