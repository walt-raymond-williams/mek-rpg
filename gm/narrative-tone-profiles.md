# Narrative Tone Profiles

Use this file when live play needs a deliberate narrative voice. Tone changes the way scenes are framed, NPCs speak, and consequences are described. It does not change rules authority, campaign facts, safety boundaries, MekHQ ownership, or tactical handoff requirements.

Before play, read the active campaign's `safety-and-tone.md`. If it names a profile here, apply that profile to scene framing and dialogue until the user changes it.

## Tone Controls

Record campaign tone with these fields:

- Primary profile: the dominant voice for scene framing and dialogue.
- Secondary profile: optional supporting flavor.
- Darkness level: light, medium, hard, or bleak.
- Violence detail: clean, serious, visceral, or graphic only with explicit approval.
- Profanity: none, light, natural, or heavy.
- Humor: none, dry, rowdy, gallows, or absurd.
- Command voice: polished, military, mercenary, noble, pirate, criminal, or local civilian.
- Avoid: specific language, cliches, content, or portrayal habits to keep out of play.

## Anti-Corporate Dialogue Rule

When the player complains that dialogue sounds corporate, rewrite the line before presenting it.

Avoid corporate abstractions:

- alignment
- readiness posture
- stakeholder
- personnel concerns
- optimize resources
- ensure success
- operational flexibility
- values
- best practices
- mission-critical synergy

Prefer military and mercenary abstractions:

- lance
- bay
- watch
- patrol
- chain of command
- sortie
- contract
- retainer
- salvage
- arrears
- ammo
- armor
- heat
- spare parts
- JumpShip window
- DropShip clock
- pay chest
- dead weight
- walking wounded
- green pilots
- bad intel
- House leash

Test: if the line could appear in a corporate leadership email, rewrite it into something a tired officer, tech chief, or lance sergeant would say under pressure.

## Rough Mercenary Military

Use for hard-bitten BattleTech mercenary outfits, especially Sharpe's Strikers.

### Ethos

The unit is not a clean patriotic command. It is a paid fighting outfit with pride, debts, jokes, grudges, habits, and ghosts. Its people fight for contract money, reputation, survival, the unit patch, and the person beside them more than for any House flag.

### Scene Texture

- Make scenes concrete: machine oil, stale coffee, sweat, cheap liquor, burnt insulation, hot armor, cramped bunks, mud, dust, and busted actuators.
- Put pressure in money, time, parts, ammo, fatigue, reputation, employer lies, bad maps, salvage disputes, and whether the machine will start when the shooting does.
- Let soldiers be rowdy, profane, superstitious, funny, angry, hungover, loyal under protest, and suspicious of polished authority.
- Keep competence practical rather than inspirational.

### Command Voice

Commanders and senior crew use military shorthand and specific stakes. They may be controlled, funny, cruel, protective, or exhausted, but they are not HR managers.

Good command lines usually do one of these:

- give a plain order
- name the immediate risk
- expose an ugly tradeoff
- cut through a lie
- threaten a consequence
- ask a pointed question
- remind the unit what is owed

Avoid generic inspiration. Use hard particulars.

Weak:

> We need to maintain operational readiness and align our expectations with the employer.

Better:

> Count the machines that can still take a hit. The Liaos bought a recon raid, not our funeral, and I want to know which one they are trying to pay for.

Weak:

> Morale may become a concern if losses continue.

Better:

> The barracks are too quiet. That is not discipline. That is people wondering whose bunk gets packed next.

Weak:

> We should preserve flexibility for changing mission conditions.

Better:

> Keep one lance loose. If this contract starts smelling worse after landing, I want teeth showing before they know we drew them.

### NPC Variation

- Commander: short, cutting, specific, burdened by pay, reputation, and casualties.
- XO or senior sergeant: tired, direct, protective of the unit, allergic to speeches.
- Tech chief: blunt about parts, time, damage, and which officer is asking for a miracle.
- Young officer: tries to sound hard and sometimes overdoes it.
- Political liaison or noble patron: polished language with knives underneath.
- Old mercenary: sparse words, ugly truths, no wasted breath.

### Avoid

- sanitized professionalism
- therapy-speak unless a specific character would use it
- movie-trailer hero lines
- nonstop grimdark hopelessness
- copying lyrics, novels, or source text
- making every soldier identical

## Sharpe-Like Military Adventure

Use when the campaign should feel like soldierly adventure with ambition, class pressure, command politics, and battlefield reputation.

- Focus: proving worth, hostile superiors, soldiers' competence, patronage, pay, and public shame.
- Voice: direct, dry, martial, sometimes funny, rarely sentimental.
- Pressure: orders from above, bad supply, enemies in uniform, disputed authority, and whether courage can survive command politics.
- Failure: humiliation, lost trust, dead soldiers, damaged standing, and missed chances.
- Avoid: spotless heroes, clean institutions, and easy speeches about honor.

## Outlaw-Country Fatalism

Use as a secondary flavor, not as a lyric imitation.

- Focus: inherited trouble, debt, bad roads, hard bargains, defiance, regret, and rough loyalty.
- Voice: plainspoken, wounded, funny, sour, and spare.
- Pressure: money owed, mistakes that followed someone across systems, ugly family or unit history, and the sense that the past is never really paid off.
- Failure: scars, broken trust, an empty bottle, a dead friend, a lost machine, or a job that pays but stains the hands.
- Avoid: quoting songs, writing parody lyrics, or making every line poetic.

## BattleTech Mercenary Realism

Use when the scene should emphasize the Inner Sphere's practical mercenary layer.

- Focus: contracts, salvage, transport, repair clocks, payroll, faction leverage, personnel losses, and reputation.
- Voice: grounded, operational, specific, and suspicious.
- Pressure: employer betrayal, bad intel, spare parts, armor loss, ammo cost, JumpShip timing, and whether the unit can still fight next month.
- Failure: debt, legal exposure, lost salvage, damaged machines, angry patrons, and battlefield disadvantage.
- Avoid: lore dumps that do not change the immediate choice.
